## Constitutional Law Analysis with Multiple Jurisdictions

### I. Introduction

This comprehensive legal analysis examines the evolution of constitutional rights across multiple jurisdictions, incorporating precedents from the United States Supreme Court, European Court of Human Rights, and Commonwealth courts.

### II. US Constitutional Framework

The foundational principle established in **Marbury v. Madison**, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803), established judicial review as a cornerstone of American constitutional law. This principle has been consistently applied in subsequent decisions including:

- **Brown v. Board of Education**, 347 U.S. 483 (1954) - establishing equal protection in education
- **Miranda v. Arizona**, 384 U.S. 436 (1966) - protecting Fifth Amendment rights during custodial interrogation
- **Roe v. Wade**, 410 U.S. 113 (1973) - recognizing privacy rights under substantive due process

Recent Supreme Court decisions have refined these principles:

1. **Obergefell v. Hodges**, 576 U.S. 644 (2015) - extending marriage equality nationwide
2. **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008) - interpreting Second Amendment individual rights
3. **Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission**, 558 U.S. 310 (2010) - expanding corporate speech rights

### III. Federal Circuit Court Developments

Federal appellate courts have provided additional guidance:

- **United States v. Nixon**, 418 U.S. 683 (1974) established limits on executive privilege
- **New York Times Co. v. United States**, 403 U.S. 713 (1971) addressed prior restraint on publication
- **Tinker v. Des Moines Independent Community School District**, 393 U.S. 503 (1969) protected student speech rights

Lower federal courts have also contributed significantly:

- **Griswold v. Connecticut**, 381 U.S. 479 (1965) recognized privacy rights in contraception
- **Loving v. Virginia**, 388 U.S. 1 (1967) struck down anti-miscegenation laws
- **Brandenburg v. Ohio**, 395 U.S. 444 (1969) refined the clear and present danger test
